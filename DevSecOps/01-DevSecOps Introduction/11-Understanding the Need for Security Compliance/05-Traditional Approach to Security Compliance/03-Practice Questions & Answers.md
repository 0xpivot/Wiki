## Practice Questions & Answers

**Q1. Explain the traditional approach to security compliance and how it resembles the waterfall methodology.**

The traditional approach to security compliance involves a linear sequence of steps, similar to the waterfall methodology used in software development. In this approach, security practices include a series of gated controls that must be passed before moving to the next step. The process starts with a risk assessment, followed by various tests and activities, culminating in a regular audit. This linear progression can lead to significant delays and inflexibility, making it difficult to integrate security seamlessly into modern agile development cycles.

**Q2. Why does the traditional approach to security compliance create friction and delays in project delivery?**

The traditional approach to security compliance creates friction and delays because it is often seen as an afterthought. Security is treated as a once-off point-in-time assessment rather than an ongoing process integrated into the development lifecycle. This leads to issues being identified too late in the project, when they are costly and time-consuming to fix. Additionally, the lack of skilled security professionals exacerbates the problem, as there are not enough experts to handle the volume of work required to ensure compliance.

**Q3. How does the traditional approach to security compliance impact the cost and speed of retesting?**

The traditional approach to security compliance significantly impacts the cost and speed of retesting. Because security is often treated as a separate phase at the end of the development cycle, any issues found require retesting, which can be both expensive and time-consuming. This retesting process is further complicated by the fact that security experts are in short supply, leading to bottlenecks and delays. As a result, the overall cost and time required for retesting are higher, contributing to the perception that security is too slow and costly.

**Q4. Describe how automating security practices can address the challenges posed by the traditional approach to security compliance.**

Automating security practices can address the challenges posed by the traditional approach to security compliance by integrating security into every stage of the development lifecycle. Automation allows for continuous testing and monitoring, enabling issues to be identified and addressed early and efficiently. This reduces the need for costly and time-consuming retesting later in the process. Furthermore, automation can help scale security efforts to meet the demands of modern development cycles, ensuring that security is not an afterthought but an integral part of the development process.

**Q5. How do recent real-world examples, such as CVEs and breaches, illustrate the shortcomings of the traditional approach to security compliance?**

Recent real-world examples, such as the Equifax breach (CVE-2017-5638), highlight the shortcomings of the traditional approach to security compliance. In the case of Equifax, the company failed to patch a known vulnerability in a timely manner, leading to a massive data breach. This incident underscores the issue of treating security as a once-off check rather than an ongoing process. Had security been integrated continuously into the development and maintenance processes, the vulnerability might have been addressed sooner, potentially preventing the breach. This example illustrates the importance of adopting a more proactive and integrated approach to security compliance.
