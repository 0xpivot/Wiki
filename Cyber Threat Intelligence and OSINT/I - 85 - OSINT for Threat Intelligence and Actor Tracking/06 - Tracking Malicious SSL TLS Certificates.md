# 85.06 Tracking Malicious SSL TLS Certificates

## Introduction to SSL/TLS in Cyber Threat Intelligence

Secure Sockets Layer (SSL) and Transport Layer Security (TLS) are cryptographic protocols fundamental to modern web security, designed to encrypt communications between a client and a server. However, in the context of Cyber Threat Intelligence (CTI), these protocols serve a dual purpose. While they protect legitimate user data, they are also extensively abused by Threat Actors (TAs) to encrypt malicious traffic, hide Command and Control (C2) communications, and establish deceptive infrastructure for phishing campaigns. 

Because the creation, issuance, and deployment of TLS certificates generate metadata that is globally logged and indexed, tracking malicious TLS certificates has become one of the most reliable and high-fidelity methodologies for hunting and tracking adversary infrastructure. By analyzing certificate fields, analysts can pivot from a single indicator of compromise (IoC) to an entire network of malicious servers.

## The Anatomy of an X.509 Certificate for OSINT

To effectively track malicious infrastructure, an analyst must deeply understand the fields within an X.509 TLS certificate. Every field presents a potential pivot point.

### Crucial Certificate Fields
1. **Subject Name (Common Name - CN):** The primary domain or entity the certificate represents. TAs often use typosquatting here (e.g., `update-microsoft.com`).
2. **Subject Alternative Name (SAN):** An extension that allows a single certificate to secure multiple domains or IP addresses. TAs frequently make operational security (OPSEC) errors by including backend development IPs, internal hostnames, or other campaign domains in the SAN field.
3. **Issuer Name:** Identifies the Certificate Authority (CA) that issued the cert. Let's Encrypt is heavily favored by TAs due to its automated, free nature. However, self-signed certificates or obscure CAs can also be strong indicators.
4. **Validity Period (Not Before / Not After):** The timeframe the certificate is valid. Correlating the "Not Before" timestamp with domain registration dates and initial attack telemetry can help establish an accurate timeline of the adversary's operational preparation.
5. **Certificate Fingerprint/Thumbprint (SHA-1/SHA-256):** A unique cryptographic hash of the entire certificate. This is the ultimate pivot point. If an adversary reuses the same certificate file across multiple C2 servers, searching the internet for this fingerprint will instantly reveal all of them.
6. **Serial Number:** A unique identifier assigned by the CA or generated by the server. Custom self-signed certificate generation scripts used by malware often produce predictable or static serial numbers.

## How Threat Actors Exploit TLS Certificates

Adversaries leverage TLS certificates across several stages of the cyber kill chain:
- **C2 Infrastructure Encryption:** Tools like Cobalt Strike, Metasploit, Empire, and Sliver default to using TLS to encrypt traffic between the compromised endpoint and the listening server. If the operator fails to modify the default certificate profiles (Malleable C2 profiles in Cobalt Strike), the infrastructure becomes highly fingerprintable.
- **Phishing and Credential Harvesting:** Hosting a phishing page without a valid TLS certificate will trigger severe browser warnings, rendering the attack useless. TAs must acquire valid certificates for their deceptive domains to display the trusted "padlock" icon to victims.
- **Payload Hosting and Malware Delivery:** Serving secondary payloads over HTTPS prevents network-level intrusion detection systems (IDS) from conducting deep packet inspection (DPI) and matching the payload against known signatures.

## Certificate Transparency (CT) Logs

Certificate Transparency is an open framework requiring CAs to log all publicly trusted TLS certificates to publicly accessible, append-only cryptographic logs. The initial goal was to prevent the misissuance of certificates. For CTI analysts, CT logs act as a real-time intelligence feed of newly established infrastructure.

### Leveraging CT Logs for Threat Hunting
Tools like `crt.sh` and Certstream allow analysts to search and monitor these logs in real-time. By monitoring for specific string patterns or typosquats associated with their organization, an analyst can detect a phishing domain within minutes of the certificate being issued, often before the TA has operationalized the domain for an attack.

## Identifying Malicious Patterns and Fingerprinting

### 1. Default and Predictable Issuer/Subject Names
Many off-the-shelf C2 frameworks generate default self-signed certificates upon installation. If deployed without modification, these defaults are easily found using internet-wide scanners.
- **Cobalt Strike (Default):** Subject: `C=US, S=CA, L=Santa Clara, O=Cobalt Strike...`
- **PoshC2 (Default):** Subject: `C=US, ST=Minnesota, L=Minnetonka, O=PoshC2...`

### 2. JARM and JA3S Fingerprinting
Beyond the certificate itself, the TLS handshake can be fingerprinted.
- **JA3S:** Fingerprints the Server Hello packet (passively). It hashes the TLS version, cipher suites, and extensions selected by the server, often uniquely identifying a specific malicious server configuration.
- **JARM:** An active scanning tool that sends multiple unique TLS Client Hello packets and hashes the server's specific responses. A default C2 server behind a specific reverse proxy will yield a unique JARM hash that can be queried in Shodan.

### 3. Anomalous Expiration Dates
Self-signed certificates generated by automated scripts often possess absurd validity periods, such as exactly 10 years (3650 days) or 99 years. These anomalies stand out during global internet scans.

## Real-World Attack Scenario

### Tracking APT Infrastructure via Reused Cobalt Strike Certificates

**The Setup:**
A Tier-1 SOC analyst detects an anomalous, encrypted beaconing pattern from an internal HR workstation to an external IP address (`198.51.100.45`) over TCP port 443. The traffic cannot be decrypted, but the analyst extracts the TLS certificate presented by the server during the initial handshake.

**The Investigation:**
1. The analyst inspects the certificate and notes it is a self-signed certificate with the Subject Name `CN=sys-update-deploy.net`.
2. The analyst extracts the SHA-256 fingerprint of the certificate: `E1A2B3C4D5E6F7...`
3. The analyst pivots by querying this fingerprint in Censys and Shodan.
4. The global search reveals that this exact certificate fingerprint is currently hosted on 4 other IP addresses globally:
   - `198.51.100.45` (Initial indicator)
   - `203.0.113.88` (Hosted on a bulletproof hosting provider)
   - `192.0.2.15` (Known VPS provider)
   - `104.21.34.4` (Cloudflare CDN IP, indicating proxying)
5. Expanding the hunt, the analyst uses the JARM hash of the initial server and discovers 30 additional servers matching the exact TLS configuration, uncovering a massive, previously unknown infrastructure network operated by a known ransomware affiliate.

By pivoting on a single reused TLS certificate, the organization proactively blocked the entire network before secondary payloads could be deployed.

## Tools for SSL/TLS OSINT

1. **Censys (censys.io):** The premier search engine for querying global IPv4 space for specific certificates, SANs, or JARM hashes. Allows for complex boolean queries.
2. **Shodan (shodan.io):** Excellent for finding exposed infrastructure and correlating certificates with specific open ports and banners.
3. **crt.sh:** The standard web interface for searching Certificate Transparency logs. Excellent for finding typosquats.
4. **JARM (Salesforce):** A python utility to actively scan and fingerprint a server's TLS configuration.
5. **PassiveTotal (RiskIQ):** Crucial for historical pivoting. It allows analysts to see which certificates were associated with an IP address months or years in the past.
6. **OpenSSL / Jq:** Command-line utilities for locally parsing, decoding, and extracting fields from captured `.pem` or `.der` files.

## Automating Certificate Monitoring

Mature intelligence teams automate the monitoring of CT logs.
- **Certstream API:** Analysts consume the real-time websocket feed of CT logs using Python.
- **Regex Matching:** Scripts continuously evaluate newly issued domains against regular expressions designed to catch variations of the organization's brand name.
- **Automated Response:** Upon a match, an alert is generated in a SIEM or SOAR platform, and the domain is automatically added to the organization's Secure Web Gateway (SWG) blocklist.

## Mitigation and Defensive Countermeasures

- **JA3/JA3S Blocking:** Implement network detection and response (NDR) solutions capable of blocking specific JA3/JA3S hashes associated with known malicious tooling.
- **Strict Certificate Validation:** Ensure internal endpoints strictly validate certificate chains and reject self-signed certificates by default, preventing initial C2 establishment.
- **Brand Protection Services:** Utilize commercial threat intelligence services to continuously monitor CT logs for brand abuse and initiate rapid takedown requests.
