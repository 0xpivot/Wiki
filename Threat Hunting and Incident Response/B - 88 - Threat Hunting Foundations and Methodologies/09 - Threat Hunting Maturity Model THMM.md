# 88.09 Threat Hunting Maturity Model (THMM)

## 1. Introduction to the THMM
Threat hunting is not a binary state; an organization does not simply flip a switch and instantly possess a mature, proactive hunting operation. It is a continuous journey that requires evolving people, processes, and technology. To measure, guide, and benchmark this evolution, the security community—largely pioneered by SANS and David Bianco—developed the **Threat Hunting Maturity Model (THMM)**.

The THMM provides a framework for organizations to evaluate their current proactive detection capabilities, identify operational gaps, justify security budgets, and build a strategic roadmap for improvement. It ranges from Level 0 (no hunting) to Level 4 (cutting-edge, automated hunting).

## 2. The Five Levels of Maturity

### 2.1 Level 0: Initial (Reactive)
**Characteristics:**
- **Description:** At this level, the organization does not perform any proactive threat hunting. Security operations are 100% reactive.
- **Tools:** Basic antivirus, traditional firewalls, and perhaps an un-tuned SIEM that acts merely as an expensive log repository.
- **Process:** The security team only acts when an alert is generated by an automated system (e.g., AV detects a known virus) or a third party notifies them (e.g., law enforcement calls to say company data was found on the dark web).
- **Data Collection:** Minimal to none. Logs are kept strictly for compliance (e.g., PCI-DSS, HIPAA), not for security analysis or rapid query performance.

### 2.2 Level 1: Minimal (Indicator-Driven)
**Characteristics:**
- **Description:** The organization has begun rudimentary hunting, but it is entirely dependent on external Cyber Threat Intelligence (CTI).
- **Process:** Hunters receive a report about a new threat group, extract the static Indicators of Compromise (IoCs) like IP addresses, domains, and file hashes, and search their SIEM to see if those indicators exist in their environment.
- **Pyramid of Pain Alignment:** Focused exclusively on the bottom tiers (Hashes, IPs, Domains).
- **Limitations:** The team is constantly looking in the rear-view mirror. They can only find known threats that have already been documented by someone else. They are blind to zero-days and customized, targeted attacks.

### 2.3 Level 2: Procedural (Hypothesis-Driven)
**Characteristics:**
- **Description:** This is where true, proactive hunting begins. The organization no longer relies solely on IoCs. Instead, hunters formulate hypotheses based on adversary Tactics, Techniques, and Procedures (TTPs).
- **Process:** A hunter might hypothesize: "If an attacker compromises our web servers, they will likely use living-off-the-land binaries (LOLBins) like `certutil.exe` or `bitsadmin.exe` to download secondary payloads." They then write queries to look for this specific behavior, regardless of what IP the payload comes from.
- **Data Collection:** The organization has robust, centralized log collection (EDR, Sysmon, Zeek) with proper retention policies allowing for deep historical searches.
- **Pyramid of Pain Alignment:** Focused on the top tiers (Tools, TTPs).

### 2.4 Level 3: Innovative (Data Analysis & ML)
**Characteristics:**
- **Description:** The hunting team generates their own operational procedures and heavily utilizes advanced data analysis techniques, rather than just searching for logical patterns.
- **Process:** Hunters use statistical baselining, anomaly detection, data stacking (long tail analysis), and machine learning to find deviations from normal behavior. They are not just looking for "known bad"; they are looking for the mathematically "unusual."
- **Output:** The hunters regularly create new, highly effective detection rules based on their successful hunts and feed them back into the automated SIEM alerts, improving the overall posture.
- **Tools:** Jupyter Notebooks, Python (Pandas/NumPy), ELK stack, Splunk Machine Learning Toolkit (MLTK).

### 2.5 Level 4: Leading (Automated & Optimized)
**Characteristics:**
- **Description:** The zenith of threat hunting. The organization has successfully automated the majority of the data processing and routine hunting tasks.
- **Process:** The focus is on *Detection as Code*. Hunts that were manually executed in Level 3 are now automated scripts that run continuously. The human hunters spend their time researching cutting-edge, zero-day threat vectors, mapping them, and building new automated systems to detect them.
- **Feedback Loop:** Seamless integration between Threat Intel, Threat Hunting, Detection Engineering, and Incident Response. If a hunt finds something, the mitigation and detection deployment is automated via CI/CD pipelines (e.g., using Sigma rules).

## 3. Assessing and Advancing Maturity

Organizations cannot jump from Level 0 to Level 4 overnight. Progression requires strategic, targeted investments in specific areas:
- **Level 0 to 1:** Implement a SIEM, purchase a CTI feed (or integrate open-source feeds like MISP), and train analysts on basic SIEM query languages.
- **Level 1 to 2:** Deploy EDR across the enterprise, deploy Sysmon, map defenses to the MITRE ATT&CK framework, and teach analysts how to formulate testable hypotheses.
- **Level 2 to 3:** Hire data scientists or train hunters in Python and data analytics. Establish robust environmental baselines for networks and endpoints.
- **Level 3 to 4:** Implement SOAR (Security Orchestration, Automation, and Response) platforms, adopt Detection as Code methodologies (e.g., Sigma rules managed in Git), and focus heavily on continuous metric optimization.

## 4. Real-World Attack Scenario: Maturity in Action

### The Scenario: Supply Chain Compromise (e.g., SolarWinds)
An advanced threat actor inserts a backdoor into a legitimate, digitally signed software update.

- **Level 0 Response:** The organization remains completely unaware until months later when the vendor announces the breach on the news. The organization then waits for their AV vendor to release a patch.
- **Level 1 Response:** The organization reads the initial CTI report, grabs the published IP addresses of the C2 servers, and searches their firewall logs. If the attacker used different IPs for their specific network, the Level 1 team finds nothing and assumes they are safe.
- **Level 2 Response:** The hunters hypothesize that the compromised software might spawn unusual child processes. They query the SIEM for `ParentImage=solarwinds.exe` spawning `cmd.exe` or initiating strange outbound network connections. They successfully detect the anomaly.
- **Level 3 Response:** The team runs a routine anomaly detection script that looks for deviations in service behavior. The model flags the SolarWinds executable because its network connection frequency and destination ASN deviated significantly from its 6-month historical baseline. They catch the breach *before* any public disclosure.
- **Level 4 Response:** The anomaly is detected automatically by continuous scripts. A SOAR playbook instantly queries the endpoint to grab the anomalous binary in memory, sandboxes it, confirms malicious behavior, isolates the host, and generates a new Sigma rule—all before the human hunter even pours their morning coffee.

## 5. ASCII Diagram: The Threat Hunting Maturity Journey

```text
       [ Level 4: Leading ] ----------------> (Focus: Automation, CI/CD, Zero-Day Research)
              ^
              | (Automate manual hunts, implement SOAR, CI/CD for detections)
              |
       [ Level 3: Innovative ] -------------> (Focus: Statistics, Machine Learning, Baselines)
              ^
              | (Learn data science, establish baselines, learn Jupyter/Pandas)
              |
       [ Level 2: Procedural ] -------------> (Focus: Hypotheses, MITRE ATT&CK, TTPs)
              ^
              | (Deploy EDR/Sysmon, learn behavior-based hypothesis generation)
              |
       [ Level 1: Minimal ] ----------------> (Focus: Threat Intel, IoCs, Hash/IP Matching)
              ^
              | (Deploy SIEM, ingest intel feeds, basic searching)
              |
       [ Level 0: Initial ] ----------------> (Focus: Reactive, Alert-driven, Fire-fighting)
```

## 6. Key Metrics for Measuring Hunting Success
To prove ROI and maturity progression, organizations must track metrics:
1. **Hunt Success Rate:** The percentage of hunts that lead to a new detection rule or find actual malicious activity.
2. **Coverage of MITRE ATT&CK:** The percentage of techniques for which the organization has validated detection capabilities.
3. **Detections Deployed:** The number of automated rules generated as a direct result of manual hunt operations.
4. **Data Quality Index:** A measurement of log health (e.g., "Are we receiving Sysmon Event ID 1 from 100% of endpoints?").
